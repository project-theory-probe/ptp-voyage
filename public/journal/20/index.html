THE ARCHITECTURE OF INTIMACY

Kitty Gia Ngân

In my previous writing on diffractive intimacy, I described a mode of perception called environmental awareness—a way of sensing in which everything is in motion, indeterminate, and without fixed position or hierarchy. In this awareness, we attune to our relational fields without a separate self.

To understand the architecture of this non-separate self, I want to introduce Deleuze and Guattari’s theory of assemblage and the rhizome. Their work gives language to what I observe: (1) intimacy flourishes as rhizome, not roots, (2) intimacy is an assemblage while relationship is a solid stratification. We need to understand these differences—and learn to move between them.

What is an assemblage?
  • Assemblage is a constellation of heterogeneous elements linked together temporarily to do something.
  • Example: Like a project or a probe, which is living in the realm of fluid multiplicities, not yet reduced into categories, functions, and identities like an organization.
  • There’s no linear order, no hierarchy.
What is stratification?
  • Stratification happens when assemblages become rigid, organized, hierarchically structured.
  • Example: A tree is linear, with roots as the most essential, forming a tree-body and replaceable leaves. Many top-down organizations are literally imagined as a tree.
  • Binary logic, biunivocal relationship, or fascicular system. Exist dichotomy and hierarchy.

Intimacy is an assemblage, while 'interpersonal relationship' is stratification.

  • Intimacy is an assemblage: temporary, heterogenous, indeterminate, no goals or structure.
  • Relationship is a stratification: it’s intimacy becoming solid, rigid, hierarchized over time, organized with rules, fixed roles, identities, and expectations.
  • Assemblages tend to become stratified, which then deterritorialize intimacy.
  • Our modern relationships are a constant process of solidifying (stratifying with rules, expectations, roles, identities) and disrupting (intimate experiences by being in the state of fluidity, openness, involvement).
  • Intimacy cannot live in fixed identities and hierarchies and dichotomies.

WHAT’S LOVE* GOT TO DO WITH IT?

Kitty Gia Ngân

Building architectures for intimacy.

A metaconversation on intimacy as a revolution.

I love architectural designs. I love observing buildings, doodling them and thinking about them.

I will write about my experience with the university project in Vietnam, the political climate here where there are rich businessmen who are also devout buddhists, and the struggles we/I faced in navigating a project about love while using the same language of violence. I think designs are important to create new conditions and patterns of intimacy that help us understand love.
