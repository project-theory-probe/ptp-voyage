WHAT’S LOVE* GOT TO DO WITH GIFTING?

Kitty Gia Ngân

I was torn between many possible topics for the last journal of PTP season 2. Just yesterday, I was certain I wanted to write about monsters and the cracks they open. I’ve even been diving into decolonial studies to prepare for it. And yet, these days, I can’t ignore an irresistible pull: a deep, overflowing sense of gratitude for the gifts I’ve received. I want to dedicate the last zine on love sharing my joy and reflections on gifting.

In this month’s zine, The Reason for Gifting, I reflect on the “rules” of gifting:

  • A gift naturally arises when you feel so abundant that you want to give back or give forward. Such motivations don’t arrive from a sense of scarcity. If it does, what looks like a “gift” is actually an obligation, a moral drama, or a bargain.
  • There’s always empathy lurking in a gift — a shared joy and/or shared sorrow. A gift cannot happen without affect-ion. To give is to let oneself be affected by an-other. And to be affected, one must choose to look long enough, deep enough, and feel. A gift without empathy is a transaction or moralised charity.
  • The urge to give and give back is irresistible, like the rush of a waterfall or the warmth of the sun rays. When you surrender to it, the boundary between giving and receiving dissolves, so is the boundary of “you” and “I”. Gifting becomes a practice that transcends separations, and it’s experienced through the feeling of shared joy.

I find our universe imbued with wonders and gifts—in a sweet plum, laughter, tender dialogue. I feel so enriched that my joy spills over, untameable. It pours out in what people call “creativity” or “art”. But I’m simply just obeying the irresistible pull to create: To me, creativity is simply gifting in reciprocity. For some people, this creative gifting takes a form of cooking, teaching, coding, singing, philosophising, and so on.

To say, “I am creating a gift for you”—and to have it received—is, to me, among the greatest honors imaginable.
