THE ARCHITECTURE OF INTIMACY

Kitty Gia Ngân

Over the past year, I am not the author of this article and its insight—it has always been crafted together and I participated in its articulation. We went far and reached impossible places, which precisely is the point of probing and voyaging. I call us friends and our activity friending. My work simply boils down to friendship*—*I do things with friends, because of friends, for my friends. I find folk practice to be most aligned with this intimate mode of becoming.

In contrast to scientific practice—where knowledge is derived from an objective, universalised, repeatable method, or capitalistic habit—where production is made for a universal, scalable market, folk practice comes from knowledge-making and craft within one’s own community (1). It’s local, special, and intimate. Whereas science and capitalism operate on a logic of subtraction (removing context to make results and products repeatable), folk practice operates on a logic of addition and connection, contaminated (2) by relations across space-time-matter.

Folks and friends must be understood across space-time-matter. The term folks includes our ancestors, authors (3)*,* minerals, plants, teachers (4), aspirations, ideas and so on. It cannot be limited to human-centric bodies. And secondly, they are reciprocal.

In journal 18, we made a breakthrough and introduced the Diffractive Model of Intimacy (5), this contrasts with the dominant Interpersonal Model of Intimacy. In such (autistic) dance of perception, friendship is not about the relationship between humans, but a state of becoming contaminated, where the cosmos meets itself and opens up cracks that diverge from the normative scripts (6). To go far, one has to get contaminated, get affected by folks and friends one has not imagined welcoming in before.

2. On gift and bazaar — to inhabit a new reality.

“I try to print a world into existence that is as welcoming and nurturing as the universe is to me.” — Amos Paul Kennedy Jr., Citizen Printer

If a person traces the minor lines that deviate from dominant strata, how do they sustain it from being reterritorialised by the dominant norms, structures, and logics that define what is legible, valuable, or “normal” in our given society? (7)

I want to answer this question above by re-articulating shortly the ideas I’ve learned from Haruma and Shotaro on the topic of gifting ontology and bazaar.

Gift-ontology and bazaar are lines of flight—ways of breaking out of the everyday captures that reterritorialise life into dominant norms of productivity, recognition, and legibility.

The Architecture of Intimacy and the PTP journals introduce minor lines—delicate trajectories that are easily overwritten by majoritarian codes. Gifting and bazaar sustain these minor lines. Gift-ontology shifts affect from scarcity and separation toward a recognition of friendship, entanglement, and intimacy, while bazaar is a style of decentralised co-creating, a platform where gifts can proliferate. Together, they create assemblages in which environmental perception, relational ontology, and diffractive intimacy are not pathologised or neutralised but given room to flourish.

Ending season 2, I hope our readers and colleagues the assemblage that allow you to join lines of flight that allow your life the intensity, passion, freedom, and joy that your heart knows is possible. What we create in PTP is always a friend of such ventures. And I look forward to test a gifting bazaar in my own life. The warmest of luck!
